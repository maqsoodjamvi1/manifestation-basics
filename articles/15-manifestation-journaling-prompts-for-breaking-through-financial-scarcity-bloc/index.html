Most people think financial scarcity is about the balance in their bank account. In my experience, it’s actually about the internal narrative that runs on a loop every time a bill hits my inbox or a luxury item catches my eye. If you grew up hearing “money doesn’t grow on trees” or “we can’t afford that,” those scripts become the blueprint for your adult financial life.

Journaling isn’t just about writing down goals. It is a way to “debug” your subconscious. When I started treating my journal like a data-gathering tool rather than a diary, I began to see where I was actively pushing money away.

Here are 15 prompts designed to dismantle those blocks and replace them with a more practical, expansive relationship with money.

Identifying Your Hidden Money Scripts

We often have “money ghosts”—inherited beliefs that dictate our spending before we even realize what’s happening. These prompts help you drag those ghosts into the light.

  1. What is the most common phrase my parents said about money, and how do I catch myself repeating it when I swipe my debit card?
  2. If I had to describe my relationship with money as a human relationship (e.g., a toxic ex, a distant parent, an untrustworthy friend), who would it be and why?
  3. What is the one purchase I’ve been putting off for months, and what is the specific fear—not the cost—that keeps me from clicking “buy”?
  4. List three “safe” ways I currently shrink my financial life to avoid the responsibility of having more money.
  5. If money were an energy source, am I currently hoarding it out of fear, or am I flowing it toward things that actually improve my quality of life?

Shifting from Scarcity to Capacity

Scarcity is a contraction. It’s the feeling of holding your breath. To manifest more, you have to prove to your nervous system that you can handle, manage, and enjoy more.

  1. Write about a time you received money unexpectedly. How did you feel in the 10 minutes after you got it? Did you spend it immediately to get rid of the “anxiety” of having it, or did you savor it?
  2. Describe a $500 expense that would make my daily routine 20% more efficient. Why do I feel guilty about wanting that efficiency?
  3. What does “financial freedom” look like on a Tuesday afternoon when no one is watching? Be specific: What am I eating, where am I working, and who am I talking to?
  4. List five ways I am currently “rich” in time, health, or community that have nothing to do with my bank balance. This builds the foundational feeling of abundance.
  5. If I were a person who easily attracted $10,000 in extra income this month, what is the first boring, administrative habit I would change about how I track my finances?

Practical Rewiring Exercises

Manifestation is often misunderstood as “wishing.” I view it as aligning your daily actions with your desired reality. These prompts bridge the gap between abstract feelings and concrete decision-making.

  1. Look at my last three impulse buys. What emotional void was I trying to fill, and what could I have done instead to meet that emotional need for free?
  2. Write a letter to my “future wealthy self.” What is the biggest mistake they tell me they’ve already learned to avoid?
  3. If I were to donate or invest 10% of my income starting today, what would that force me to change about my current spending habits?
  4. Identify one “leaky” financial habit (like recurring subscriptions I don’t use or late fees I keep paying) and write down the exact step I will take to plug that hole by Friday.
  5. What is the biggest “what if” that scares me about making more money? (e.g., “What if I become a greedy person?” or “What if people stop liking me?”)

How to Make These Prompts Work

Don’t just write these answers and move on. The “magic” happens in the follow-up. For example, if you realize in prompt 14 that you are paying for two streaming services you never watch, cancel them. That action creates a physical signal to your brain that you are no longer a person who “leaks” resources.

Be honest with yourself. If you feel a physical tightness in your chest while answering a prompt, that is a sign you’ve hit a genuine block. Don’t gloss over it. Write about that tightness. Describe it. Ask it what it’s trying to protect you from. Usually, you’ll find that your “scarcity mindset” is actually a misguided attempt by your brain to keep you safe from change.

Once you have identified these blocks, you can begin to make small, deliberate choices that move you toward your financial goals. Recognizing the patterns is the hardest part; changing them is simply a matter of consistent, small pivots.

The next step is to take the insights you’ve gathered from these exercises and turn them into a simple, automated financial strategy that supports the person you are becoming. By aligning your habits with your new mindset, you move from “trying” to manifest to actually living in an abundance-focused reality.
