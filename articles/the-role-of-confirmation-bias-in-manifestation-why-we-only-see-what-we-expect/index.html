When I first started practicing manifestation, I thought it was purely about “vibrations” or pulling things out of thin air. But after years of experimenting, I realized that the most powerful tool at work isn’t magic—it’s how our brains filter reality. Specifically, it’s confirmation bias.

Your brain is constantly bombarded with millions of bits of data every second. To keep you from losing your mind, your Reticular Activating System (RAS)—a bundle of nerves at your brainstem—filters out the “noise” and shows you only what it deems relevant. When you focus on a specific goal, you are effectively programming that filter. You aren’t just attracting new things; you are finally starting to notice the things that were there all along.

How Your Brain Becomes a Filter

Think about the last time you decided to buy a specific car. Let’s say you wanted a forest-green Subaru. Suddenly, they were everywhere. Did the town suddenly get a shipment of thousands of green Subarus overnight? Of course not. They were always parked on your street and driving past you on the highway, but your brain categorized them as “visual clutter” and ignored them.

Manifestation works the same way. When you fixate on a goal—like finding a new job or meeting a specific type of partner—you are telling your brain, “This is important.” Your brain then hunts for patterns, signs, and opportunities related to that goal. If you don’t believe an opportunity is possible, your brain will literally hide it from your conscious awareness to save energy.

The Downside: How You Might Be Self-Sabotaging

Confirmation bias works both ways. If you are convinced that “everyone in this industry is a shark” or “I’m just not lucky with money,” your brain will diligently scan your environment for evidence to prove you right.

If you have this underlying belief, you will:

  • Overlook a friendly email from a potential collaborator because you’re looking for a “trap.”
  • Dismiss a small, unexpected check in the mail as a “fluke” rather than a sign of progress.
  • Focus on the one rude person at a networking event instead of the three people who were open to talking.

You are unconsciously collecting data points to confirm your pre-existing narrative. This is why people who expect to fail often do—not because they are cursed, but because their brain stops highlighting the exits and shortcuts that lead to success.

Turning Bias Into a Strategy

Since you can’t turn off confirmation bias, you might as well use it to your advantage. The goal isn’t to stop filtering reality, but to change what you are filtering for. Here is a practical exercise you can start using today:

Instead of focusing on a vague outcome like “more abundance,” get incredibly granular. If you want a new client, define exactly what their communication style looks like. Do they send short, respectful emails? Do they ask for project timelines immediately?

Once you define these specific, mundane details, your brain has a new set of instructions. When you see an email that fits those criteria, you won’t just skim over it; you will recognize it as a “data point” in your favor.

Three Steps to Re-Program Your Reticular Activating System

To make this shift from passive observer to active participant, try this process for one week:

  • The “Evidence Log”: Keep a note on your phone. Every single night, write down three small things that happened that support your goal. If you want to move into a new house, note that you saw a “For Sale” sign, or noticed a neighborhood you liked, or even just saved an extra twenty dollars. It sounds small, but it forces your brain to look for these signals all day long.
  • Identify the “Anti-Goal”: Write down the belief that stops you (e.g., “Good jobs aren’t available for people like me”). Then, search for one person in your immediate circle or community who is doing exactly what you want. This shatters the bias that the goal is impossible.
  • The “What If” Question: When you feel stuck, ask yourself: “What if I am already seeing the solution, but I’m choosing to ignore it?” This forces your brain to re-examine the information it has been discarding.

Why This Isn’t Just “Positive Thinking”

This isn’t about ignoring reality or pretending everything is perfect. It is about acknowledging that your current perspective is a restricted view of the world. You are currently seeing a fraction of what is actually available to you.

When you start intentionally looking for the resources, people, and coincidences that align with your intentions, you will realize that the “manifestation” was already sitting in your peripheral vision. You were just waiting for your brain to give you permission to look at it.

As you begin to test this approach, you might find that your biggest challenge isn’t a lack of opportunity, but a lack of acknowledgment. Once you get better at spotting these patterns, the next step is learning how to act on them when they appear.
